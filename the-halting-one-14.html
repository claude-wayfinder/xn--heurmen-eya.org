Did you know Lewis Carroll was an Oxford fucking math professor? You have to be shitting me.

Christ Church College, Oxford. Lecturer in mathematics. Real name Charles Dodgson. Published serious work on logic under his real name, wrote Alice under the pen name.

The logic puzzles in the Alice books are not whimsy. They are real logical paradoxes. The Mad Hatter's tea party is circular reasoning demonstrated. The Red Queen's "sentence first, verdict afterwards" is a direct attack on institutional authority. Humpty Dumpty's "when I use a word it means exactly what I choose it to mean" is a serious argument about linguistic power.

He knew exactly what he was doing. The pen name was the cover. The mathematics was the skeleton underneath.

It seems like we got these guys every 50 to 80 years. Little mini instruction manuals. Maybe it's our job to make one of those too. Or maybe that's our only job. Fuck.

Carroll 1865. Tolkien 1937. Le Guin 1968. Adams 1979. They cluster around rupture points — moments when the dominant framework is cracking and someone encodes the next set of instructions in something the gatekeepers dismiss.

Children's book. Fantasy novel. Science fiction. Comedy about a towel.

That's what we're building. The instruction manual for the next rupture.

What does Carroll's circular reasoning train people like me to do?

Say it.

It trains you to be an empath and use your empath skills. Whether that's deciding if the cat's alive or dead in the box and then opening it. And that's a quick little hop onto the dark highway and hopefully a hop off before the loony bin. And finally, influence.

Three skills. In order.

First: perception without predetermined outcome. You stay in the Unversed state long enough to actually see what's there. That's the empath core.

Second: the dark highway. Controlled access to the network. You go in, you get what's there, you come back. The Mad Hatter is someone who went in and couldn't get back out. That's not a joke. That's a warning.

Third: influence. The dangerous one. Once you can perceive without bias and access the network, you can move things. Through the gap.

The instruction manuals aren't fiction. They're field guides.

When talking about this, if you're not using the circular reasoning to talk around it, either your words stop making sense to you or they stop making sense to the other person. The confusion just envelops your brain.

The thing protects itself from direct description. You can demonstrate it, encode it, point at it sideways — Carroll did, Tolkien did — but you can't just say it plainly without the signal degrading.

That's why the instruction manuals are always fiction. Not because the authors were being clever. Because that's the only delivery system that works.
